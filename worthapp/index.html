My Commitment to Me

Focus on my needs

Choose an issue in your life that you would like to work on. Try to think of a goals that you can set today that will help you along your road to health and wellness.

Take a moment to think about people you can reach out to for help. Consider resources available to you to help you reach your goal. For example, your local clinic can help you get medical care. A probation or parole officer might help you find resources. Your state may have a toll free information hotline you can call to find out more about the services in your community.

If you are completing WORTH in a group, your group facilitator can provide help in accessing the services you may need.


What kind of support do you need?


Setting a Goal

You're all set right now. That's great!

What goal can you set to find help? Write down your goal and your plan to reach it on your worksheet.

Here are some suggestions to get you started:
  • Use the internet, local library, or a toll free information hotline like 311 to gain information about services in your community
  • Talk to people in my social support network for suggestions and advice
  • Go to my local college to see if they have information sessions
  • Find and call my local clinic
  • Discover if I am eligible for assisted healthcare by applying online, by mail, or by contacting a social worker or local agency
  • Speak with a social worker or parole officer
  • Volunteer at local organization or agencies that assist formerly incarcerated individuals
  • Look into local support groups for substances that will match my needs
  • See if I am eligible for food related social services by applying online or through a mail-in application
  • Get in touch with a social worker or local agency specializing my your benefit needs
  • Look into what services are offered at my spiritual center
  • Get in touch with the spiritual advisor of my choice to see if they are open to meeting
  • Set reminders for myself on your phone, notebook, alarms etc.

Myths and Facts

True or False?

You can get HIV by kissing someone who has it.

Oops! You missed this one.
The answer is False. You cannot get HIV by kissing someone who has it. Saliva may contain HIV but not in sufficient quantities to transmit the virus. HIV, the virus that causes AIDS, is spread through unsafe sex and needle or syringe sharing where blood is passed.
Well done!
The answer is False. You cannot get HIV by kissing someone who has it. Saliva may contain HIV but not in sufficient quantities to transmit the virus. HIV, the virus that causes AIDS, is spread through unsafe sex and needle or syringe sharing where blood is passed.

True or False?

Medications today can greatly reduce the risk of transmitting HIV if positive or getting HIV if you are negative. If you are HIV+ and take your medicine consistently, you are less likely to pass on the virus during sex.

Oops! You missed this one.
The answer is True. Antiretroviral therapy slows down HIV replication in your body and is the most important way to keep yourself and your partners healthy. It is not a cure but can greatly reduce the risk of transmitting the virus to your sexual/drug partners. Even with antiretroviral therapy, it is important to consistently use condoms.
Well done!
The answer is True. Antiretroviral therapy slows down HIV replication in your body and is the most important ways to keep yourself and your partners healthy. It is not a cure but can greatly reduce the risk of transmitting the virus to your sexual/drug partners. Even with antiretroviral therapy, it is important to consistently use condoms.

True or False?

If you have recently been exposed to HIV there is no reason to take medication for it.

Oops! You missed this one.
The answer is False. You can reduce the risk of becoming HIV positive by taking medications within 48 hours following exposure. This is called post-exposure prophylaxis or PEP.
Well done!
The answer is False. You can reduce the risk of becoming HIV positive by taking medications within 48 hours following exposure. This is called post-exposure prophylaxis or PEP.

True or False?

As long as they are not injected, it is safe to share needles and "works" with others.

Oops! You missed this one.
The answer is False. Sharing needles and other works when preparing to inject drugs increases risk of HIV infection.
Well done!
The answer is False. Sharing needles and other works when preparing to inject drugs increases risk of HIV infection.

True or False?

It is easy to tell that you have chlamydia.

Oops! You missed this one.
The answer is False. You may not know that you are infected with chlamydia because you may not notice symptoms. You may be infected and experience no symptoms. In fact, for many STIs, such as chlamydia, most people transmit the infection without experiencing any symptoms. Chlamydia also has lasting effects and can lead to pelvic inflammatory disease, ectopic pregnancy, and infertility among women.
Well done!

It is not possible to pass sexually transmitted infections to unborn children.

Oops! You missed this one.
The answer is False. A mother can pass gonorrhea to her unborn child. Gonorrhea can be passed on to the child during birth. It can cause blindness in the newborn.

True or False?

If you do not have any symptoms, it is not possible to pass on HSV-2 (genital herpes) to my partner.

Oops! You missed this one.
The answer is False. You do not have to have an outbreak or other symptoms in order to be infectious and pass HSV-2 (genital herpes) to a partner. HSV-2 (genital herpes) shedding is responsible for genital herpes transmission whether these episodes lead to recognized outbreaks or are clinically asymptomatic. In fact, up to 70% of transmissions can be attributed to asymptomatic shedding.

True or False?

HPV (genital warts) causes cancer.

Oops! You missed this one.
The answer is True. HPV causes cervical cancer. Women should get an annual exam to receive a pap test to screen for evidence of HPV (genital warts) infection. HPV is easily transmissible and the most prevalent STI, particularly among young women. All boys and girls ages 11 or 12 years should get vaccinated. Catch-up vaccines are recommended for males through age 21 and for females through age 26, if they did not get vaccinated when they were younger.
Well done!

True or False?

Having an untreated STI can increase the risk for acquiring HIV.

Oops! You missed this one.
The answer is True. Having an STI changes the cells lining the vagina, penis, rectum or mouth. This makes it easier for HIV to enter your body. If you already have an STI, you are MORE likely to get infected with HIV if you have sex with someone who has HIV and you don't use a condom.
Well done!

True or False?

Most viral STIs cannot be cured.

Oops! You missed this one.
The answer is True. Once your body is infected with an STI, your body will always carry it.
Well done!
The answer is True. Once your body is infected with an STI, your body will always carry it.

True or False?

Being high or intoxicated increases the risk of HIV/STI infection.

Oops! You missed this one.
The answer is True. Being high or intoxicated increases the risk of HIV/STI infection.
Well done!
The answer is True. Being high or intoxicated increases the risk of HIV/STI infection.

Learn More

Using non-injected drugs like alcohol, marijuana, crystal meth, or crack cocaine reduces one’s ability to make good decisions about safe sex or using clean needles or works. Being intoxicated or high decreases one’s ability to think about protecting oneself from HIV/STIs. Cocaine tends to increase a person’s sex drive and to a degree reduces sexual inhibitions which can make condom use less likely and increases the likelihood of engaging in high risk sexual activities. Crystal meth has an effect similar to cocaine and it appears to be leading to increased unprotected sex and to greater HIV/STI transmission.

Source: UF Behavioral Science and Community Health

True or False?

Many more men than women have HIV.

Oops! You missed this one.
The answer is False.
Well done!
The answer is False.

Learn More

Women now make up about half of all the people living with HIV and new cases of HIV in the world.

Source: PACT/Connect "Facts about HIV/STI Risks"

True or False?

New York City has the most HIV cases in the United States.

Oops! You missed this one.
The answer is True.
Well done!
The answer is True.

Learn More

NYC has the highest number of HIV cases in the country. In addition, 1 in 3 people living with HIV in NYC are women, of which 90% are African American.

Source: PACT/Connect "Facts about HIV/STI Risks"

True or False?

More women in prison and on probation are HIV positive than men.

Oops! You missed this one.
The answer is True. More women in prison and on probation are HIV positive than men.
Well done!
The answer is True. More women in prison and on probation are HIV positive than men.

Learn More

In 2004, one of every 42 women in prison was diagnosed as HIV positive, compared to 1 of every 59 men. In addition, 12% of men on probation in NYC are reported to be HIV positive. In a study of 306 women on probation in NYC in 2012, 14% were found to be HIV positive.

Sources:

  • The Sentencing Project, Women in the Criminal Justice System, May 2007
  • PACT/Connect "Facts about HIV/STI risks" sheet

If someone experiences a traumatic event and afterwards experiences unwanted thoughts, continues to relive what initially happened, feels disconnected from things that used to make them feel better or safe, and feels isolated or numb, name the type of stress disorder they may be experiencing.

Correct
Unwanted thoughts, reliving the event, disconnection and numbness are all symptoms of PTSD. Someone experiencing this should seek help if they can. It is possible to recover and feel better.
Incorrect
Someone experiencing these symptoms is not just in a bad mood or feeling down. Unwanted thoughts, reliving the event, disconnection and numbness are all symptoms of PTSD. Someone experiencing this should seek help if they can. It is possible to recover and feel better.
Incorrect
These symptoms are all markers of high levels of stress experienced by someone with Post Traumatic Stress Disorder (PTSD). Someone experiencing this should seek help if they can. It is possible to recover and feel better.

Which symptoms of trauma did Sofia experience? Select all that apply.

Correct, Sofia experienced all the symptoms of trauma listed here.

Extreme emotions: After a traumatic event, people often experience a wide range of emotional, physical and stress responses, often at the same time. Some reactions may appear totally unrelated but oftentimes can overwhelm our ability to cope emotionally and physically

Difficulty sleeping: In response to a trauma, many may experience changes to their sleep pattern. They may have difficulty sleeping for long periods of time or may even withdraw and find it difficult to get up out of bed each morning.

Using drugs to try to cope and forget: After experiencing a traumatic event, many may start drinking or start using drugs as a way to try to deal with the extreme emotions they experience.

Zoning out at work: A normal sign of trauma includes having uncontrollable thoughts and unintentionally distancing oneself from others and one’s surroundings.

Having constant memories of what happened: Many women who experience an extremely traumatic event may develop post-traumatic stress disorder, known as PTSD. One sign of PTSD involves having repeated unwanted thoughts or reexperiencing the initial traumatic event.

Reducing Risks

2.24

Session 2: Taking in the View

Goals: Reducing Risks

When you set goals to reduce the risks to your health you are putting yourself first! Get out your goals worksheet or a piece of paper, write down a goal, and write down a plan to make it happen. Here are some suggestions to get you started:

  • Buy a pack of condoms
  • Increase my use of condoms
  • Talk with my partner(s) about using condoms
  • Reduce the number of partners I have
  • Encourage my partner(s) to get tested for HIV and/or HCV
  • Encourage my partner(s) to get treated for HIV, HCV, and/or other STIs
  • Speak with a doctor to find out more about PrEP or PEP
  • Make an appointment to see my HIV care provider
  • Get treatment for an STI
  • Take my HIV medication, Antiretroviral Therapy (ART), as prescribed
  • Get the medication I need to treat my STIs
  • Not have sex after drinking or taking drugs
  • Not share needles or unclean injection equipment with anyone
  • Make the daily commitment to my recovery from alcohol and substance misuse

Goals: Reducing Risks

When you set goals to reduce the risks to your health you are putting yourself first! Get out your goals worksheet or a piece of paper, write down a goal, and write down a plan to make it happen. Here are some suggestions to get you started:

  • Buy a pack of condoms
  • Increase my use of condoms
  • Talk with my partner(s) about using condoms
  • Reduce the number of partners I have
  • Encourage my partner(s) to get tested for HIV and/or HCV
  • Encourage my partner(s) to get treated for HIV, HCV, and/or other STIs
  • Speak with a doctor to find out more about PrEP or PEP
  • Make an appointment to see my HIV care provider
  • Get treatment for an STI
  • Take my HIV medication, Antiretroviral Therapy (ART), as prescribed
  • Get the medication I need to treat my STIs
  • Not have sex after drinking or taking drugs
  • Not share needles or unclean injection equipment with anyone
  • Make the daily commitment to my recovery from alcohol and substance misuse
